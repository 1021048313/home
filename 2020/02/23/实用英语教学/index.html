reading: how the brain handles language

the brain consists of several anatomically distinct regions:

  1. cerebrum大脑
    1. largest
    2. be divided into two lobes by the brain stem
  2. cerebral cortex 大脑皮质
    1. is the area primarily involved in the control of voluntary movement and intellectual functions
    2. in the decoding of information from the senses
    3. the surface of it is not smooth,but has folded in on itself to produce a series of convolutions(gyri)
  3. convolutions 脑回
    1. are separated by fissures,or sulci 脑沟
  4. fiber tracts 纤维束
    1. is beneath the cortex
    2. transmits signals between the different parts of each hemisphere,and between the cortex and the brain stem
  5. hemispheres
    1. be connected to the spinal cord by the brain stem
  6. brain stem脑干
    1. consists of the mid-brain,the pons,the medulla oblongata
  7. pons脑桥
    1. cerebellum is at its back
  8. cerebellum小脑
    1. is responsible for the maintenance of body posture and the smooth coordination of all movements

The main feature of the brain is the median longitudinal fissure(中枢神经系统).The whole way through the cerebrum is the means whereby information can be transmitted from one hemisphere to the other.

The central sulcus(中央沟)(the fissure of Rolando) and the lateral sulcus(外侧沟)(the sylvian fissure) are the main features,which are used as criteria for dividing the brain into four main lobes: frontal,temporal, parietal, and occipital.

Each hemisphere controls movement in and receives sensory input from the opposite side of the body. So he left hemisphere controls the movement of the right side of the body, and vice versa. And if one hemisphere were damaged, the body would get bad.

For instance:

  1. ears: signals from each ear go to both hemispheres, but most information goes to the opposite side.
  2. eyes: the left half of the visual field of each eye transmits information to the right hemisphere, and vice versa.

Dominance

Each of the two hemispheres has its own role. Dominance means leading some functions,and such functions are known as lateralization(偏侧优势)

The left hemisphere is dominant for language in most right-handed people(over 95%),however,left-handers are homogeneous. And in over 60% cases the left hemisphere is either dominant for language or very much involved.

With right-handed people,the left hemisphere is found to be dominant in such activities as analytical tasks,categorization,calculation,logical organization,information sequencing,complex motor functions,and language. While the right is said to be dominant for the perception and matching of global patterns,part-whole relationships,spatial orientation,creative sensibility,musical patterns,and emotional expression or recognition.

Localization

Damage to specific areas if the cortex correlated with the loss of certain kinds of linguistic ability to speak:

  • to Broca’s area: a reduced ability to speak, comprehension remained relatively unimpaired.
  • to Wernicke’s area: a reduced ability to comprehend speech,though the ability to speak was relatively unaffected.

Neuro-linguistic processing

Any model of the production and comprehension of language involves several steps–which can be delineated by neuro-linguistic models.

Some research has been done,for example: it is evident that the brain does not issue motor commands one segment at a time. The word”soup” is not neurologically transmitted as three separate steps -[s]+[u]+[p],showing that the brain must be ‘scanning ahead’ while issuing commands for particular segments.

One kind of evidence

The analysis of slips of the tongue are very common: sounds,syllables,words,and sometimes other units of grammar.

The tongue slips are not random,but are largely explicable by reference to certain basic constraints. And combining such constraints,it is possible to make predictions about the form tongue slips are likely to take when they occur.

article: how we describe consonants and vowels

Describing consonants

Four criteria

  • their place of articulation in the vocal tract
  • manner of articulation in the vocal tract
  • the state of vibration of the vocal folds
  • the position of the soft palate

Varying the place of articulation

Two reference points defining where we make consonants

  • the part of vocal tract that moves (the active articulator)
  • the part towards which it moves or with which it makes contact (the passive articulator)

11 possible places are used in speech

  • bilabial–both lips
  • labiodental– lower lip
  • dental–make the tongue tip and rims articulate with the upper teeth
  • alveolar–alveolar ridge
  • postalveolar or palato-alveolar–alveolar ridge,at the same time raising the front of the tongue towards the hard palate
  • retroflex–curl the tip pf the tongue back to articulate with the area between the rear of the alveolar ridge and the front of the hard palate.
  • palatal–the hard palate
  • velar–soft palate
  • uvular
  • pharyngeal
  • glottal

Varying the manner of articulation

Four main kinds of constriction ,made by the articulators in producing consonants:

  • total
  • partial
  • narrowed
  • intermittent

Consonants which make a total closure

  • plosives
  • nasals
  • affricates

consonants which make a partial closure

Laterals: We make a closure at one location in the mouth. but in such a way that we allow the air to escape around the sides of the closure.

Consonants which use a narrowing

Fricatives: We make two vocal organs come so close together that the movement of air between them causes audible friction. The term fricatlve reﬂects the nature of the sound produced.

Consonants which make an intermittent closure

Rolls or Trills: tap rapidly against another

Flaps: produce a single tap against another

Describing vowels

We usually describe vowels in four criteria:

  • the part of the tongue that is raised -front, center, or back.

  • the extent to which the tongue rise; in the direction of the palate. three or rout degrees: high, mid and low . we can describe tongue height as close, mid-close, mid-open and open.-

  • the position oi the sot palate — raised for oral vowels, and lowered for vowels which have been nasalized; and–

  • the kind or opening made at the lips- various degrees of lip rounding or spreading.

Introduction to Articulatory Phonetics (Consonants)

This is the study of how speech sounds are produced in the vocal tract,focusing on how consonant sounds in North American English are produced in vocal tract.

What’s the difference between consonants and vowels?

  • Consonants involves some construction of airflow,whereas vowels do not.

When linguists described consonant sounds,we use three criteria:

  • voicing(state of the glottis)

    referring to what the vocal folds are doing

    When air passes through open vocal folds–voiceless sounds[p] [s]

    When air passes through vibrating vocal folds–voiced sounds[b] [z]

    feeling the differences: putting hands on the adam’s apple position

  • place of articulation

    referring to where in the vocal tract the construction of airflow takes place

    bilabial sounds are produced with both lips [p] [b] [m]

    labiodental sounds are produced with the upper teeth and the low lips [f] [v]

    interdental sounds are produced with the tongue in between the upper and lower teeth [θ] [ð]

    alveolar sounds are produced with the tongue at or near the ridge right behind upper front teeth [t] [d] [s]

    palatal sounds are produced at the hard palate or the roof of the mouth [ʃ] [ʒ] [j]

    velar sounds are produced at the velum or soft palate [k] [g]

    glottal sounds are produced at the glottis or the space between the vocal folds [h]

  • manner of articulation

    referring to how the airflow is constricted in the vocal tract.

    stop sounds result from a complete constriction of airflow followed by a release of that air [p] [t] [k] [b] [d] [g]

    fricatives are sounds produced when the tongue approaches but does not make contact with a place of articulation causing a bottleneck of the airflow [θ] [v] [z]

    affricate results from the sequence of stop plus fricative in rapid succession [dʒ]

    nasal sounds are produced when the velum is lowered allowing air to pass through the nasal cavity [m] [n]

    liquid sounds are produced by allowing air to pass by one or both sides at the tongue and the tongue itself can move a lot to shape the sound [l]

    glide sounds are produced with very little constriction of air flow,they are often referred to as semi-vowels [w] [j]

    tap sounds are involving rapid flick of the tongue to some place of articulation. In North American English,we often have only one tap at the alveolar ridge(牙槽棱线) [butter]

Introduction to Articulatory Phonetics (Vowels)

Two types of vowel sounds:

  • Monophthongs

    involve one vowel quality

  • Diphthongs

    involve two vowel qualities

Criteria for describing vowels:

  • height

    how high or low the tongue is in the mouth when producing the vowel

    vowels are classified as high,mid,low

  • backness

    how far front or back the tongue is when producing the vowel

    front,central,back

  • roundedness

    whether or not the lips are rounded when producing the vowel

Grammarians divided the range of construction into sub-fields,often the two:

  • the study of the grammatical structure of words(morphology)
  • the study of the grammatical structure o sentences(syntax)

and below the article will be separated into these two parts

morphology

Intro

The term dates from the early 19th century.
Two main fields are traditionally recognized within morphology

  • inflectional morphology studies the way where words vary in order to express grammatical contrasts in sentences
  • derivational morphology studies the principle governing the construction of new words,without reference to the specific grammatical role.

Analyzing words

  • words have no internal structure capable of carrying meaning–analyze them into their constituent sounds
  • free morphemes–occur on the word only.
  • bound morphemes–depend on others
  • the structure of a word: root,base,stem

Some morphology problems

  • It is difficult to know how to analyze irregular nouns and verbs
  • morphemes sometimes have several phonetic forms,depending on the context where they occur

Identifying words

some ways:

  • adding pauses
  • adding extra words
  • minimal free forms
  • listening for phonetic boundaries
  • identifying semantic units of meaning

article: how sentences work

Definition: to show relationships of meaning within or between sentences,because this is where the most important grammatical relationships are expressed.

**Origin: ** The term comes from syntaxis,the Greek word for arrangement.

Traditional sentence grammar: ‘the complete expression of a single thought’ was depressed, because of the difficulties involved in saying what ‘thoughts’ are.

Logical definition: ‘a sentence has a subject and a predicate’,but does not work well for all sentences.

Punctuation: ‘begin with a capital letter and end with a full stop’,but it does not work for some other languages,and it is not a clear guide. What’s more,it is difficult to identify sentences in speech.

Finding hierarchy One of the first things to do in analyzing a sentence is to look for grouping within it.

clauses: have two major units-each of which has a structure closely resembling that of an independent sentence

Displaying sentence structure: immediate constituent(IC) analysis. It works through the different levels of structure within a sentence in a series of steps. At each level, a construction is divided into its major constituents.

To make an IC analysis of the sentence,we carry out the following steps:

  • Identify the major constituents
  • Divide the next-biggest constituent into two
  • Continue dividing constituents into two until we can go no further(somewhat like binary tree traversal)

Conclusion:

  • We need more than an analysis of an individual sentence.
  • We also need to devise a way of showing how sentences relate to each other grammatically-as with statements and questions.
  • In general linguistics,we need to devise representations which can be used to analyze the grammar of any language

article: Received Pronunciation

Received Pronunciation(RP.) is regarded as the most respectable accent in England.

often associated with the south-east of England,where most RP-speakers live or work.

tell us about a speaker’s social or educational background

development:

  • Middle Ages,in London and the south-east,as the accent of the court and the upper class

  • In 1589, Elizabethan courtier George Puttenham agreed with it,with some one holding on their local speech,but most worried for adopting London accent. As a result,the accent soon came to symbolize a person’s high position in society.

  • During the 19th century,it became the accent of public schools,soon the main sign of high education. It spread rapidly throughout the Civil Service of the British Empire and the armed forces(the”Army Dreamers” by Kate Bush sounds really great:face_with_head_bandage:) ,and become the voice of authority and power

  • For it had few regional overtones and was more widely understood than other accents(such as in CN,north accent is more understood,so I can hear about my roommates’ calling with families easily,but they can’t do the same thing on me),it came to be adopted by the BBC in the 1920s for broadcasting.

    During the WWⅡ,it stands for freedom,the terms RP and the BBC English became synonymous.

  • These days,with the break down of rigid divisions between social classes and the development of the mass media,it is now best described as an educated accent(s) with several varieties.

  • But there are two forms of RP:

    • the trend-setting variety,often described as ‘far back’ ‘frightfully,frightfully’-the ‘Sloane Ranger’ accent of the 1980s
    • the more conservative variety id old-fashioned,referred as a rather ‘plummy’ tone of voice.

  • Now most people speak ‘ modified’ RP-a mixture of RP and various regional characteristics

  • RP continues to be a widely used accent in the Court,Parliament,the Church of England,the legal profession,and in other institutions. It has received more linguistic research than any other accent.
